Redemption Centers in New York are Closing While the Bottle Bill Gets Postponed

Redemption centers are closing because of the economic difficulties they face since they have not received a raise for the work they do for 40 years. The new Bottle Bill would double the payment currently received by recyclers and redemption centers in New York State.

By: Ana Maria Betancourt

December 20, 2024

Redemption centers have been closing massively since 2022 because of economic struggles.

The new Bottle Bill proposes to raise the deposit fee from 5 cents to 10, and the handling fee from 3.5 to 6 cents. This would double the current revenue of the state's independent redemption centers and recyclers.

If it is approved, the distributors will have to invest more money in recycling the containers of the products they sell. But according to the Container Recycling Institute the main opponents of the bill are companies like PepsiCo, Coca Cola and Anheuser Busch.

In 2023 the bill was discussed in the New York State Assembly, but it did not pass. It will be discussed again in the next legislative session on January 28th, 2025.

Jade Eddy, owner of MT Returnables redemption center located in Queensbury, NY, and member of the Empire State Redemption Association , is thinking about closing her business because she doesn't have the money to stay afloat.

“I am currently receiving poverty assistance, I spent all my retirement savings and lost my house to try to keep my business afloat,” Eddy said with disappointment. “The position they have put us in is criminal because I see legislators in Albany more concerned about big companies like Pepsi, who oppose the Bottle Bill, than about the small businesses that serve their communities.”
